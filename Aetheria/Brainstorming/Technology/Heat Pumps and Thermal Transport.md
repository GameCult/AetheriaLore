[[Cooling Beyond Blackbody]] solves only the final interface between hot machinery and cold vacuum. Before waste heat can be rejected, it has to be spread out from hotspots, collected into loops, routed across hulls or habitats, and sometimes lifted to a higher rejection temperature. In practice that means thermal management is not one miracle technology but a stack. Whoever controls the stack can decide which ships overclock safely, which habitats can hide industrial density, and which factions keep living with furnace districts and mandatory derating.

### Research Anchor

The real spacecraft literature suggests that "heat pumps" in space are not one thing. They are a family of transport and lift technologies used for different jobs.

- NASA's 2025 small-spacecraft thermal-control survey treats heat pipes, cryocoolers, thermoelectric coolers, and fluid loops as distinct thermal-control layers.
- Loop heat pipes use evaporation, condensation, and capillary wicks to move heat without external pumping power and are already baseline spacecraft technology.
- NASA and JPL work on multi-evaporator loop heat pipes shows that one network can share heat loads across multiple components and multiple condensers.
- Pumped fluid loops are the workhorse answer when many hot zones need to dump heat to one or more radiators.
- Thermoelectric coolers are true miniature solid-state heat pumps, but they are mainly useful for localized cooling and lose practicality at large temperature differences or very low temperatures.
- Reverse turbo-Brayton cryocoolers sit at the more extreme end of active heat lifting. NASA has studied them for cryogenic storage and broad-area cooling because they can both refrigerate and circulate coolant in the same loop.

The main lesson is that moving heat sideways across a vehicle is often more mature and more important than "pumping" it in the everyday sense. Temperature lift exists, but it costs power, complexity, and maintenance. That makes the transport layer its own industrial empire.

### What The Stack Looks Like

- Spreading: thermal straps, spreaders, and local phase-change structures pull heat out of components before they cook themselves.
- Transport: heat pipes, loop heat pipes, and pumped loops carry that heat across meaningful distances to shared sinks.
- Lift: thermoelectrics or cryocoolers raise the effective rejection temperature or hold a component below ambient loop temperature when needed.
- Rejection: radiators, louvers, deployable fins, and exotic emitters dump the final load to space.

The rejection layer can itself split into very different traditions. A brute-force ship might feed heat into red-hot refractory panels or liquid-droplet radiators. A premium Cryonix-dependent ship might feed a smaller but much more complex extraordinary-emitter skin. The circulation problem remains central in both cases.

This distinction matters for setting design. A faction might own miraculous emitter surfaces and still depend on someone else for wick fabrication, working-fluid chemistry, compressor reliability, valve logic, contamination control, leak management, and loop routing.

### Why This Should Not Belong Entirely To Cryonix

[[Cryonix]] should still dominate the glamorous end of the problem: advanced thermal sinks, lattice materials, and the acquired emitter breakthrough that turns radiator surfaces into strategic assets. But the transport and lift side feels structurally harder to monopolize.

It is less about one miracle material and more about ugly system integration. Pumps fail. Loops cavitate. Wicks clog. Microchannels foul. Control software oscillates. Bearings wear. Valves seize. A company can own the best radiator face in Sol and still depend on another firm to get the heat there without vibration, leakage, thermal runaway, or absurd parasitic power draw.

That helps keep [[Cryonix]] powerful without making it absurdly universal. It can be the king of rejection surfaces while still bargaining with specialists in thermal circulation and lift.

### Silent Running and Heat Debt

Thermal transport is also what makes stealth credible. A ship cannot truly hide by making heat vanish. It hides by not releasing heat yet. That means storing heat in phase-change masses, thermal batteries, sacrificial sinks, or cold propellant; isolating parts of the loop with thermal switches; and using shutters, louvers, or routing tricks to decide when and where the eventual rejection happens.

Real spacecraft thermal-control technology already contains pieces of this logic. NASA's thermal-control survey includes phase-change thermal storage units for slowing temperature spikes, thermal switches that alternate between strong and weak coupling to sinks, and louvers that regulate how much heat a surface dissipates. In setting terms, these become not just engineering details but tactical verbs: bottle heat, isolate heat, move heat, dump heat.

That makes stealth time-limited and therefore interesting. Every quiet ship accumulates heat debt. The question is not whether it can remain invisible forever, but how long it can stay cold before it must vent, glow, or hand the heat to something else. See [[Thermal Stealth and Signature Warfare]].

### Faction Angles

- [[AstroDyne]] is a strong candidate if this side of the stack should feel rugged, repairable, and frontier-proven. Thermal transport fits its brand of systems that keep working after elegance has failed.
- [[Rossum & Douglas]] is a strong candidate if the emphasis should be insurer-approved safety, redundancy, and control stability. It could become the quiet authority behind thermal-loop certification, failure containment, and conservative heat-lift systems.
- [[NiteLife Energy]] works if habitat-scale district cooling, fusion-adjacent heat logistics, and premium thermal utilities should become part of the energy monopoly.
- [[Orbital Forge]] works if you want the transport layer to grow out of habitat plumbing, salvage improvisation, and radiator-farm architecture rather than clean laboratory invention.

One useful split is this: [[Cryonix]] owns the premium faces that reject heat, while another faction owns the circulatory system that makes those faces matter.

### Integration Possibilities

- Ship classes divide not only by engine power or armor but by how elegant their thermal circulation is. Cheap vessels can have excellent emitters on paper and still perform badly because their internal transport network cannot feed them under combat loads.
- Habitats become socially legible by thermal plumbing. The rich live near quiet high-capacity loops with forgiving margins. The poor live where pumps whine, maintenance windows slip, and every industrial surge becomes communal weather.
- Military procurement splits into radiator contractors and loop contractors, creating different lobbying blocs, sabotage opportunities, and sanctions regimes.
- Black-market or dissident engineering often focuses on transport rather than emission. A stolen Cryonix radiator skin matters less if you cannot integrate it into a stable loop.
