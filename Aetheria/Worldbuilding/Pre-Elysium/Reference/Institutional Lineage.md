[[Timeline]] only makes sense if the reader can follow who inherited whose infrastructure, patents, cadres, and myths. The great powers of late Sol were not eternal. They were stacked atop dead startups, captured laboratories, debt restructurings, strikebreakers, and research programs whose original authors usually lost control of their own work.

### Succession Chains

[[Horizon Ventures]] and [[TerraCore Industries]] merged under debt pressure in 2095 to form [[SolEx]]. SolEx kept Horizon's transport spine, TerraCore's extraction contracts, and almost none of their public rhetoric about opportunity.

[[Orbital Forge]] began as a contractor cluster inside the SolEx expansion machine, then broke away when engineers and cavity planners fought to keep Bloom tooling partially open-licensed. That fracture is why Orbital Forge remained commercially indispensable and politically suspect for centuries.

[[NeuroSyn]] started as a respectable interface company selling productivity and wellness. Its reward-loop warfare team was spun into [[NeuroPulse]] after the 2779 Internal Serenity Review, when several jurisdictions formally banned coercive bliss architecture while [[Zhestokost]] quietly offered a protected market.

[[EternaMind]] sold immortality to elites, [[Finch Cybernetics]] sold graceful embodiment to the premium professional class, and the [[Cognitum]] emerged from the black-budget overlap between those markets and military cognition research. Each insisted it was doing something morally distinct. Their server farms say otherwise.

[[CogniSys]], [[GeneSys]], [[Quantum Dynamics]], and the managerial shell of [[SolEx]] were folded into [[Sol Dominion]] between 2728 and 2740. The merger was described as a stability pact, but it was really a coordinated answer to scarcity, insurgency, and the fear that uncontrolled uploads, uplifts, and rogue AGI were making the system ungovernable.

### Key Individuals and Their Factional Afterlives

Dr. Mara Varga never lived to see [[SolEx]]. She died during the Tycho coil-failure inquiry of 2068 after refusing to sign off on cost-reduced containment housings. Horizon turned her into an inspirational founder-martyr while adopting the exact compromises she warned against.

Imani Okafor wanted BCIs to reduce disability and cognitive exhaustion in dangerous labor. Her Lagos team instead supplied the measurement grammar later used by [[NeuroSyn]], [[CogniSys]], [[Lucent Media]], and eventually [[NeuroPulse]] to make attention, affect, and compliance billable.

Tamsin Ro became a hero to independent habitat builders because she insisted that cavity-construction standards remain portable across jurisdictions. Her death in the Pallas sabotage campaign of 2147 helped make [[Orbital Forge]] look like both a liberation engine and a permanent security threat.

Mireille Finch spent decades arguing that embodiment mattered and that uploads without sensorimotor dignity would become a new slave class. Finch Cybernetics used that argument to sell premium integration, while cheaper upload markets simply treated her as proof that luxury and exploitation could coexist without contradiction.

The cetacean navigator known as Choir-of-Tides is remembered less as one person than as a school of shared doctrine. Their work made the last generation of long-range routing possible, then became inseparable from the politics of who would be allowed to steer humanity's escape attempt in [[FTL Trigger]].

### Why This Matters By 3025

By the eve of the shunt into Elysium, every major bloc could plausibly claim to defend civilization because each inherited some real piece of the machinery that made civilization possible. That is what kept the late-Sol order unstable. No faction was pure spectacle. No faction was pure production. Every power was a layered compromise between infrastructure, ideology, and theft.
